Tech moves so fast, that by the time information reaches a book, it‚Äôs often already 6 months outdated. Another way to get inroads on Twitter is to share information you find. Lots of people do that by sharing articles, but I discovered another way to leverage Twitter ‚Äì by live-tweeting talks I attend. Not just ‚ÄúHey who else is here? Turns out I was pretty good at it! At full speed, I can do about one tweet a minute during a talk, and I can go all day like that. Thanks to those live-tweeting skills, I have earned hundreds of followers, received several books / one Chromecast / one professional Axure license (yay Twitter contests), one Directorship of Marketing (UXPALA), and most recently, two Social Media co-Directorships (World IA Day 2017 and IA Summit 2017). Having a Twitter contest at an event that I‚Äôm attending is pretty much a done deal. One time I was 20 minutes late to an hour-long talk and STILL got the most tweets!

First, a few things about Twitter

The 140-character limit is a feature or a bug, depending on who you talk to. I happen to think it‚Äôs a feature, because it teaches you to be a strong, concise writer. I‚Äôve had speakers come up to me and say that my tweets communicated their ideas better than their actual presentations. (I disagree, since I obviously learned something valuable from them, but it‚Äôs always a great compliment!) Each character counts, literally.

The extra-short form and social context allows each tweet to be inimitably shareable. That means that each tweet has to be as self-contained as you can get it. Each one should contain the speaker‚Äôs handle and the event hashtag, and be a complete sentence.

In the explosion of political discourse on Twitter, long strings of self-replies, comprised of complete or numbered partial sentences (‚Äútweetstorms‚Äù), have become popular. But I advise against prioritizing it for live events, especially in numbered partial sentence form. For one, tweetstorms are meant to be stream of consciousness, and a live-event tweet should be directed.

Secondly, I‚Äôm pretty good at this live-tweeting thing, but even I can‚Äôt listen to a talk, take a photograph of a slide, compose a tweet, AND keep track of the number of tweets in a tweetstorm I‚Äôm in. You want to limit as many variables as you can.

A few general things about live-tweeting in particular

Q: Should I live-tweet this event?

If it‚Äôs an event that non-locals would find informative and interesting, then yes, I live-tweet it. They‚Äôre usually presentations (with or without slides) or speaking panels, where the amount of information coming at you is pretty dense. Nobody really needs to know minute-by-minute updates of a workshop or networking happy hour. Taking pictures of a workshop when interesting things are happening is good to do, especially to attract attention toward the event organizers and/or sponsors. But constant updates at a happy hour would be considered ‚Äúgossip,‚Äù which wouldn‚Äôt necessarily improve your professional reputation!

Another consideration is that presentations usually follow a strict outlined structure. Speakers introduce a point, give some examples to support their point, then conclude that point before moving onto the next one. Once you‚Äôve attended a few of these, you can start to sense the basic structure of a talk as you‚Äôre listening to one. Live-tweeting a presentation gets much easier after that.

It‚Äôs much harder to live-tweet a panel or a casual chat, because it‚Äôs more freeform. But well-produced panels still have a structure to them, and a good moderator will prepare the speakers and keep things organized.

The most difficult event I ever live-tweeted was a ‚ÄúTown Hall‚Äù organized for Joseph Gordon-Levitt‚Äôs HitRECord community. It was a 3-hour-long Q&A where questions were not prepared beforehand, so the answers given by the staff often meandered. A lot. I would listen to someone‚Äôs 5-minute answer, wracking my brain to find a tweetable point out of the whole spiel. But hey, I got an endorsement from the ‚Äúboss man‚Äù JGL himself afterwards, so that‚Äôs a plus!

Q: What subjects are good for me to live-tweet?

That‚Äôs up to you! Live-tweeting is infinitely easier if you have some context over what the topic of the talk is before you start. I realized this while attempting to live-tweet a JavaScript talk at UX+Dev Summit 2016. I know enough JavaScript to read it, but I don‚Äôt know what JavaScript developers would find important about updated features, because I don‚Äôt know the full extent of its current features.

The more general the subject, the more likely it is that you‚Äôll be able to understand enough to write succinctly for it. You can‚Äôt bullshit your way through an informative tweet.

In addition, the more general the subject, the more people you‚Äôll have engaging with your tweets. If I was still in molecular biology, I‚Äôm doubtful that I would live-tweet DNA repair conferences. (Yes, there are such things!) There just isn‚Äôt enough of an audience, at least an audience not already at the conference, to bother. I‚Äôll also make a bet that not many DNA repair scientists are on Twitter in the first place, because it‚Äôs so specialized and they have their own ways of furthering their work in the right circles.

The point of Twitter is to engage with others. If there isn‚Äôt a strong interest or strong community already on Twitter about your subject, your efforts won‚Äôt be recognized.

Q: What do I need to live-tweet?

The only things you absolutely need is a smartphone, a connection, and a Twitter account. Phones are much easier to take photos with, if there‚Äôs a really informative diagram or slide you want to share. However, my iPhone 5S‚Äôs battery life is pretty crap after the iOS10 update, so if it‚Äôs a multi-hour event, I bring a spare battery pack and charging cord too. Despite my high tweeting rate, my typing still isn‚Äôt that great on my phone. I feel like I spend half my time deleting and re-typing stuff. Here are the environments I‚Äôve encountered, from most common to least common, and the choices I would make:

  1. 1-3-hr in-person event, with or without wifi
    • Phone using Twitter app. Use SMS if slow
  2. All-day in-person event, with wifi
    • iPad with keyboard using Twitter app. Phone (on low power mode) for photos using Twitter app, and backup for SMS tweeting if slow. Bring a battery pack!
  3. All-day in-person event, without wifi
    • Phone (on low power mode) with SMS tweeting, avoid photos. Bring a fully-charged battery pack!
  4. Online webinar, on wifi
    • Laptop using Tweetdeck

Before each event

1. Confirm the speaker‚Äôs Twitter handle, so you can use it throughout the talk.

Speakers love support and attention! They‚Äôd want to know that people are listening to them and learning from their efforts. If you‚Äôre new to tech, now you‚Äôve put your name on the map with them. They‚Äôll also often retweet you if you write something really informative, so their followers will see what you‚Äôre doing.

You have to confirm their Twitter handle because some people have common names, and you might select the wrong handle. I have done that before, and it‚Äôs not a good look.

Some speakers don‚Äôt have their names in their handle at all, or don‚Äôt use Twitter that often. You‚Äôll have to do some sleuthing to see if you‚Äôve got the right person. 2. Along with the speaker‚Äôs handle, confirm the event hashtag, if there is one. (If not, use the organizer‚Äôs Twitter handle.)

Most large tech-related events, especially conferences, will have an event hashtag. This is the fastest way you‚Äôll get the attention of the organizers, and find other fun, opinionated people at the event too! Also, if there is a Twitter contest, you need to use the event hashtag to get counted.

For small one-time talks, there often isn‚Äôt an event hashtag. In such instances, I‚Äôll use the organizer‚Äôs handle to get on their radar. But if you tweet enough using one hashtag, it‚Äôll catch on anyway, bwahaha.

5. Fall in love with active voice and short words.
